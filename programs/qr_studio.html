Data Encoding 101

The Language of
Squares & Patterns.

QR Codes are everywhere, from restaurant menus to crypto wallets. But how does a simple grid of pixels store complex information instantly?

2D vs 1D Barcodes

Traditional barcodes (UPC) only store data horizontally (1D). QR Codes are 2-Dimensional, storing data both vertically and horizontally, allowing them to hold 100x more information.

Reed-Solomon

This mathematical algorithm allows a QR Code to be scanned even if up to 30% of it is damaged, ripped, or covered by a logo.

#1 Anatomy of a "Quick Response" Code

Invented in 1994 by Denso Wave, the QR (Quick Response) Code was designed to track automotive parts at high speed. Unlike standard barcodes that require a perfect laser line, QR codes can be scanned from any angle. This is made possible by the three distinctive squares in the corners, known as Finder Patterns.

When your phone camera sees these three squares, it instantly calculates the orientation and size of the grid. The rest of the "noise" (the small black and white dots) is actually binary data (0s and 1s) representing your text or URL.

#2 The Magic of Error Correction

Have you ever wondered why some QR codes have a logo in the middle, yet they still work? They aren't "designed" around the logo; the logo is literally destroying part of the data. This is possible thanks to Error Correction Levels (L, M, Q, H).

Low Redundancy (Level L)

Stores less backup data (7% recovery), but the dots are less dense. Best for long URLs where you need the code to look cleaner.

High Redundancy (Level H)

Sacrifices density for durability (30% recovery). Essential if you plan to put a logo in the center or print the code on a wrinkled T-shirt.
